📍
Ectopic Pregnancy
Obstetrics 🔥 High Yield
  • Classic triad: pain + amenorrhea + vaginal bleeding (only 50% have all 3)
  • MC site: ampulla 80% (97% tubal overall)
  • Leading cause of 1st trimester maternal mortality
  • MTX criteria: β-HCG <5000, sac ≤4cm, no cardiac activity, stable, no free fluid

Risk Factors

Strong: Previous ectopic • PID/Chlamydia • Tubal surgery/ligation • IUD use • Infertility • Salpingitis

Weak: ART/IVF • Age >35 • Multiple partners • Smoking (×1.6–3.5) • DES exposure

Implantation Sites (by frequency)

Ampulla 80% → Isthmus 12% → Fimbria 5% → Cornua 2% → Interstitium 2%

Earliest presentation: Isthmus (ruptures earliest, less space)

Investigations

Serial β-HCG (no doubling in 48h = abnormal) • TVUS (most important tool)

Discriminatory zone: sac visible on TVUS at β-HCG 1500–2000 mIU/mL

MTX Contraindications — ALL exclude MTX
β-HCG >5000 • Fetal cardiac activity • Free fluid in pelvis (hemoperitoneum) • Hepatic/renal/hematologic dysfunction • Breastfeeding • Immunodeficiency • Active pulmonary disease

Management

Expectant: β-HCG <1000 + declining + asymptomatic + sac ≤4cm

MTX: 1 mg/kg IM or 50mg/m². Monitor day 4 & 7 — need ≥15% decline day 4→7. Initial rise in first 3 days = NORMAL.

Salpingostomy (tube-sparing): fertility desired, other tube diseased

Salpingectomy: no fertility desire, healthy other tube, ruptured, bleeding

After Treatment
Give Anti-D if Rh-negative. Avoid pregnancy ≥3 months after MTX.

Preeclampsia & HELLP
Obstetrics 🔥 High Yield
  • BP ≥140/90 after 20wk + proteinuria ≥0.3g/24h
  • Severe: BP ≥160/110 OR symptoms/labs/IUGR + BP ≥140/90
  • MgSO4: 4g IV bolus → 1g/h × 24h. Antidote: IV Calcium Gluconate
  • HELLP: Hemolysis + Elevated LFTs + Low Platelets → deliver regardless GA

Mild PET

SBP 140–160, DBP 90–110

Proteinuria <5g/24h (+1/+2)

Urine output >500ml/24h

No symptoms/labs/fetal changes

Deliver at 37 weeks

Severe PET

SBP ≥160, DBP ≥110

Proteinuria ≥5g/24h (+3/+4)

Urine output ≤500ml/24h (oliguria)

Headache, visual disturbance, epigastric pain

Stabilize → Deliver immediately

Antihypertensives

1st line: IV Labetalol | 2nd line: IV Hydralazine | Oral: Nifedipine 10mg (max 30mg)

MgSO4 Dosing

IV: 4g bolus over 15–20 min → 1g/h × 24h

IM (painful): 10g loading (5g each buttock) → 5g q4h

MgSO4 Toxicity (in order)

Warmth/flushing → Loss of patellar reflex (FIRST sign) → Somnolence → Respiratory paralysis → Cardiac arrest

Monitor: DTRs, RR, urine output, serum Mg q6h

HELLP Management
Deliver regardless of GA • Dexamethasone 10mg IV q12h • Transfuse if platelets <20,000 • CS: transfuse if <50,000

Eclampsia Emergency Steps

1. Protect/left lateral 2. Airway + O₂ 3. IV access + Foley 4. Blood + urine labs 5. MgSO4 loading 6. Antihypertensives 7. Stabilize FIRST — do NOT deliver during acute seizure 8. Deliver when stabilized

Prevention: Low-dose aspirin 75mg from 12 weeks + Calcium supplementation

Postpartum Hemorrhage
Obstetrics 🔥 High Yield
  • Definition: >500ml VD, >1000ml CS. Leading cause maternal mortality
  • 4 T's: Tone 80% • Trauma 20% • Tissue 5% • Thrombin
  • 1st uterotonic: Oxytocin immediately after anterior shoulder
  • Carboprost (PGF2α) 80–90% effective; CI in asthma

Active 3rd Stage Management

1. Oxytocin immediately after anterior shoulder delivery

2. Delayed cord clamping

3. Gentle cord traction + uterine counter-traction (Brandt-Andrews)

Uterotonics

Surgical Escalation

Bakri balloon → UAE → B-Lynch suture → Uterine artery ligation → Internal iliac ligation → Hysterectomy

Resuscitation

2× IV gauge-14 • Crystalloids 5L per 1L loss • PRBCs if >1.5L loss

Every 5 PRBCs → 1 FFP (prevent dilutional coagulopathy) • 1U PRBC = ↑Hb 1g/dL

Secondary PPH (24h–6wk)
Usually RPOC + infection (E.coli) • Admit + IV ABx (3rd gen cephalosporin + metronidazole) + D&C under US guidance

Preterm Labor & PROM
Obstetrics 🔥 High Yield
  • Confirm PROM: speculum exam — pooling in posterior fornix
  • Steroids: 24–34+6wk — Betamethasone 12mg IM q24h × 2 doses
  • MgSO4 neuroprotection: 24–29+6wk — 4g IV → 1g/h
  • Routine ABx NOT recommended in intact-membrane PTL

Chorioamnionitis Signs (→ stop tocolysis, deliver)
Maternal HR >120 on 2 occasions 30min apart | Temp >38°C on 2 occasions 1h apart | Uterine tenderness | Offensive liquor

PPROM Management

GBS prophylaxis: Benzyl penicillin 3g IV stat → 1.5g q4h

🩸
APH: Previa & Abruption
Obstetrics
  • APH = bleeding after 24 weeks
  • Previa: painless bleeding, soft abdomen, malpresentation common
  • Abruption: painful bleeding, tender/tense abdomen, can be concealed
  • Never PV in suspected previa — risk catastrophic hemorrhage
Placenta Previa
RF: Previous previa, multiparity, previous CS, increased age, multiple gestation
Dx: Abdominal US (95%), TVUS (100%)
Stable <37wk: expectant (steroids, Anti-D, crossmatch)
Deliver at 37wk or if active bleeding
Delivery: always CS
Placental Abruption
RF: PET, previous abruption, trauma, polyhydramnios, smoking, multiparity
Dx: US + coagulation profile (DIC risk)
Complication: DIC, shock, renal failure
Management: Resuscitation → FFP for DIC → Steroids → Anti-D → Deliver
Definitive Rx: delivery

Management (both, if stable and preterm)

Wide bore IV + crossmatch • CBC • Steroids (if <34wk) • CTG • Anti-D if Rh-negative • Admission

Diabetes in Pregnancy
Obstetrics 🔥 High Yield
  • Screen at 24–28 weeks with 75g OGTT
  • GDM: 88% of all DM in pregnancy; 82–93% controlled by diet alone
  • Fetal: macrosomia, caudal agenesis (most specific), NTD, VSD
  • Delivery: controlled ≤40+6wk; uncontrolled 37–38+6wk

75g OGTT Thresholds (1 of 3 = GDM)

TimeThreshold
Fasting≥5.1 mmol/L (92 mg/dL)
1 hour≥10.0 mmol/L (180 mg/dL)
2 hour≥8.6 mmol/L (153 mg/dL)

Management Ladder

Diet + exercise (30min post-meal) → If fails in 1–2 wks → Metformin 500mg → If fails/FBS ≥7.0 → Insulin

Insulin doses: 1st trim 0.7-0.8 U/kg/d • 2nd trim 0.8-1.0 • 3rd trim 0.9-1.2

Insulin does NOT cross placenta. Metformin does (counsel).

Pre-existing DM Preconception

HbA1c <6.5% before pregnancy • HbA1c >10% → advise against pregnancy (25% anomaly risk)

High-dose folic acid 5mg for 3 months before + first 12 weeks

Aspirin 75mg from 12 weeks (PET prevention)

Postpartum
75g OGTT or FBS at 6–12 weeks • GDM → 50% lifetime T2DM risk

Rh Isoimmunization
Obstetrics 🔥 High Yield
  • Problem: Mother Rh−, Father Rh+; sensitization via FMH
  • 1st pregnancy: IgM (can't cross) → 2nd: IgG → hemolysis → hydrops
  • Anti-D prophylaxis: 500 IU at 28wk (and 34wk), within 72h of any sensitizing event
  • Best non-invasive assessment: MCA-PSV Doppler

Anti-D Doses

SituationDoseTiming
<20 weeks250 IU IMWithin 72h
>20 weeks500 IU IMWithin 72h
Postpartum≥500 IU IMWithin 72h of delivery
Additional100 IU per 1ml fetal bloodAfter Kleihauer-Betke
Indications for Anti-D
APH • ECV • Amniocentesis/CVS • Abdominal trauma • Ectopic pregnancy • IUFD • Any invasive procedure
NOT indicated
Spontaneous complete miscarriage <12wk | Threatened miscarriage <12wk that stops

Kleihauer-Betke: quantifies fetal RBCs in maternal blood using acid elution. 20 fetal cells = 1ml FMH = 100 IU extra anti-D.

Once sensitized: Anti-D will NOT help. Monitor with serial ICT titers + MCA-PSV. IUT if <34wk, deliver if >34wk.

Critical titer: ICT 8–32 = risk of fetal hydrops.

💊
VTE in Pregnancy
Obstetrics
  • DVT 90% on left (May-Thurner syndrome)
  • Drug of choice: LMWH — does not cross placenta
  • Warfarin embryopathy: 6–12wk (nasal hypoplasia, CC agenesis). Avoid antenatally except mechanical heart valves
  • D-dimer NOT useful in pregnancy (always elevated)

Risk Score → Prophylaxis

ScoreAction
≥4 antenatalLMWH from 1st trimester + 6wk postpartum
3 antenatalLMWH from 28wk + 6wk postpartum
≥2 postnatalLMWH ≥10 days

Factor V Leiden = MC thrombophilia | Antithrombin deficiency = most potent

LMWH Timing Around Delivery

Regional block: avoid for 12h (prophylactic) or 24h (therapeutic) after last dose

1st post-delivery dose: 4–6h after VD; 6–12h after CS (if no PPH)

Previous VTE: continue LMWH ≥6wk postpartum regardless of mode of delivery

Acute PE Investigation

ECG MC: sinus tachycardia | Classic: S1Q3T3 | Start LMWH immediately while investigating

DVT: compression duplex US | Suspected PE: CTPA or V/Q scan

🫧
Molar Pregnancy & GTN
Obstetrics
  • Complete: 46XX (paternal), no fetus, "snowstorm" on US
  • Partial: 69XXY (triploid), partial fetus, presents like missed abortion
  • Complete: 15–20% post-molar malignancy risk vs partial 0.5–1%
  • PET before 20wk → always exclude molar pregnancy

Complete HM Features

Vaginal bleeding 97% • Large for dates 50% • PET 27% (early onset!) • Hyperemesis 25% • Hyperthyroidism 7% • Theca lutein cysts 50%

Management

Suction curettage → Serial β-HCG surveillance → Contraception (avoid pregnancy until β-HCG normal)

If β-HCG plateau/rise after evacuation → Gestational Trophoblastic Neoplasia → Chemotherapy (MTX)

DDx for Large-for-Dates in Early Pregnancy
Complete HM • Multiple gestation | If also PET before 20wk → strongly consider complete HM

Abnormal Uterine Bleeding
Gynecology 🔥 High Yield
  • PALM-COEIN: Polyp, Adenomyosis, Leiomyoma, Malignancy | Coagulopathy, Ovulatory, Endometrial, Iatrogenic, Not classified
  • Menorrhagia: >80ml/cycle or >7 days
  • DUB: AUB with anovulatory cycles, diagnosis of exclusion
  • Postcoital bleeding → always investigate cervix first

History Points

Quantify: number + size of pads, soaked, clots, restriction of activity

Associated: IMB, PCB, dysmenorrhea, pelvic pain, pressure symptoms, discharge

Drugs: anticoagulants, tamoxifen, OCP/IUD

Family history: endometrial, breast, colon cancer

Treatment Ladder (Menorrhagia)

1. Tranexamic acid / NSAIDs (non-hormonal, 1st line)

2. COC or Progestogens

3. Mirena IUS (highly effective)

4. GnRH analogues (short-term: 6 months max, SE: menopausal symptoms, osteoporosis)

5. Endometrial ablation (CI: fertility desired, hyperplasia/cancer, fibroids)

6. Hysterectomy (100% cure rate, definitive)

🔵
Fibroids (Leiomyoma)
Gynecology
  • MC indication for hysterectomy; benign smooth muscle tumors
  • Peak symptoms 35–45 years; black race 9× more common
  • Submucosal = most symptomatic (bleeding + infertility)
  • Red degeneration in pregnancy → acute abdomen, fever, localized pain

Types (by location)

Intramural • Subserosal • Pedunculated • Submucosal • Interligamentary • Parasitic

Symptoms

Menorrhagia + anemia • Dysmenorrhea • Pressure: urinary frequency, constipation • Infertility • Recurrent miscarriage

Management

Conservative: small, asymptomatic, near menopause, during pregnancy

GnRH analogues: reduce size before surgery, improve anemia. SE: menopausal symptoms, osteoporosis (>6 months)

Myomectomy: fertility desired • UAE: alternative to surgery

Hysterectomy: completed family, severe symptoms, rapidly growing

Suspect Leiomyosarcoma
Rapid growth, large initial size, symptomatic, age 50–60 years (0.1–1% of fibroids)
🌙
Endometriosis
Gynecology
  • Secondary dysmenorrhea + deep dyspareunia + infertility
  • Examination: fixed retroverted uterus, blue nodules on speculum, adnexal mass
  • Gold standard diagnosis: laparoscopy
  • GnRH analogue SE: menopausal symptoms + osteoporosis (>6 months)

Laparoscopic Findings

Red-flame lesions (early, active) • Chocolate cysts (ovarian endometriomas) • Adhesions • Peritoneal pockets • Powder-burn (blue-black) deposits

Medical Treatment Options

COC continuously • Progestogens • GnRH analogues (max 6 months) • Danazol (androgenic SE)

Surgical

Laparoscopic ablation/excision of deposits • Drainage of chocolate cysts • Adhesiolysis • Hysterectomy + BSO (definitive)

Adenomyosis vs Endometriosis
Adenomyosis: endometrial tissue within myometrium → bulky tender uterus, congestive dysmenorrhea, menorrhagia. Usually older, parous women. Definitive Rx: hysterectomy.
👶
Infertility
Gynecology 🔥 High Yield
  • Primary (never conceived) vs Secondary (previous pregnancy)
  • First investigations: SFA + tubal patency (HSG or laparoscopy)
  • Confirm ovulation: Day-21 progesterone
  • Normal SFA: >2ml, >20 million/ml, >50% motility, >40% normal morphology

History (Structured)

Ovulatory: cycle regularity, dysmenorrhea, mid-cycle pain/discharge, PCOS symptoms, galactorrhea, thyroid symptoms, hot flashes

Tubal: previous PID, ruptured appendix, previous ectopic, pelvic surgery, septic abortion

Uterine: fibroids, D&C history, endometriosis, congenital anomaly

Male: previous fathering, testicular surgery/injury, mumps orchitis, STDs, medications

Investigations

Ovulatory: Day 21 progesterone • Hormonal profile (FSH, LH, prolactin, TFTs) • BBT chart

Tubal: HSG (CI: active PID, dye allergy) or laparoscopy + dye

Uterine: HSG (also shows uterine cavity) • Hysteroscopy

Treatment by Cause

PCOS/anovulation: Clomiphene citrate (SE: OHSS, multiple pregnancy) • Metformin

Tubal disease: Surgery (recanalization) or IVF

Male factor (oligospermia): IUI | Severe: ICSI/IVF

IVF complications: OHSS • Multiple gestation • Ectopic pregnancy

🌸
Menopause & HRT
Gynecology 🔥 High Yield
  • 12 months amenorrhea without other cause; average age 51
  • FSH >30–70 IU/L confirms menopause
  • PMB = endometrial Ca until proven otherwise → hysteroscopy + biopsy
  • HRT: Combined (E+P) if uterus intact; Estrogen alone only post-hysterectomy

Symptoms

Vasomotor: hot flashes (MC, 85%), night sweats, palpitations

Urogenital: vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, atrophic vaginitis, UTI frequency

Long-term: osteoporosis, CVD risk, dementia

HRT Contraindications

Absolute CI
Pregnancy/suspected • Breast/endometrial Ca • Acute liver disease • Uncontrolled HTN • Confirmed VTE
Relative CI
Fibroids • Benign breast disease • Unconfirmed VTE • Chronic stable liver disease • Migraine

Osteoporosis Prevention

Weight-bearing exercise • Calcium + Vitamin D

Medications: Bisphosphonates (1st line) • Raloxifene (SERM) • HRT (1st line if <50yo, POF) • Parathyroid hormone

PMB & Endometrial Cancer
Gynecology 🔥 High Yield
  • MC gynecological malignancy; estrogen-dependent
  • PMB (any bleeding >12 months post-menopause) → investigate
  • Diagnosis: US (endometrial thickness >4mm) → hysteroscopy + biopsy
  • Basic treatment: TAH + BSO + nodal sampling + peritoneal washings

Risk Factors (Unopposed Estrogen Theme)

Obesity • PCOS • Nulliparity • Early menarche/late menopause • Tamoxifen • Estrogen-secreting tumors • Endometrial hyperplasia • DM + HTN (cofactors)

PID Complications

🔻
Genital Prolapse
Gynecology
  • "Something coming down" worse at end of day/standing
  • Cystocele (anterior) • Rectocele (posterior) • Enterocele (POD) • Uterine
  • MC risk factor: childbirth/multiparity
  • Conservative: pelvic floor exercises + ring pessary (change yearly)

History Points

Cystocele: incontinence, frequency, needs to reduce manually to void

Rectocele: constipation, incomplete evacuation, manually reduces to defecate

RF: Multiparity with vaginal deliveries, menopause, obesity, chronic cough, constipation, connective tissue disease

Surgery

Uterine prolapse: Vaginal hysterectomy + repair (Manchester repair / sacrohysteropexy)

Cystocele: Anterior colporrhaphy

Rectocele: Posterior colpoperineorrhaphy

LeFort's operation: for elderly women not fit for major surgery

🔭
Cervical Disease & Pap Smear
Gynecology 🔥 High Yield
  • 99% caused by HPV (oncogenic: 16 & 18 → 70%)
  • Transformation zone (squamocolumnar junction) = where CIN develops
  • PCB → speculum exam → pap smear
  • HSIL → immediate colposcopy + biopsy → LLETZ

Pap Smear Adequacy

Adequate = contains both squamous AND columnar cells (from transformation zone)

Spatula for ectocervix + Cytobrush for endocervix

LSIL vs HSIL Management

LSIL: Repeat smear in 6–12 months OR colposcopy

HSIL: Immediate colposcopy + biopsy → if CIN 2/3: LLETZ (loop excision)

Colposcopy Findings

Acetowhite epithelium • Punctation • Mosaic pattern • Atypical vessels (invasion)

HPV Vaccine

Bivalent (HPV 16, 18) or Quadrivalent (16, 18, 6, 11) | Age 12–26 | 3 doses IM

In Pregnancy
High-grade lesions: defer treatment — CIN → cancer takes years. Repeat colposcopy postpartum.

Antepartum Hemorrhage — 32-week Primigravida
History Management
A 32-year-old G4P3, 32 weeks gestation presents to the ER with mild painless vaginal bleeding. Her previous deliveries were all by CS. Vitals are stable. How do you manage her?
1
What is your working diagnosis?
✓ AnswerAntepartum Hemorrhage (APH) with the most likely cause being Placenta Previa (painless bleeding + multiple previous CS are major risk factors for both previa and placenta accreta).
2
From history alone — how do you differentiate placenta previa from abruption? Give 5 points.
✓ AnswerPrevia: Painless recurrent bleeding • Previous previa • Multiple CS • Multiple gestation • No pain/contractions
Abruption: Painful bleeding (abdomen tender/tense) • Trauma • Smoking • Hypertension/PET • PROM • Abdominal pain precedes or accompanies bleeding
3
What investigation confirms placenta previa and why not PV?
✓ AnswerUltrasound (abdominal 95% accurate; transvaginal 100% accurate). PV is absolutely contraindicated — the examining finger could separate the placenta from the uterine wall, causing catastrophic, uncontrollable hemorrhage.
4
Patient is stable at 32 weeks. Give 4 key management points.
✓ Answer1. Admit + IV access + crossmatch 4 units blood
2. Steroids (dexamethasone/betamethasone for lung maturity — patient is <34wk)
3. Fetal wellbeing: CTG + US biophysical profile
4. Anti-D if Rh-negative (indirect Coombs negative)
5
When do you plan delivery and by what route?
✓ AnswerElective CS at 37 weeks if stable. Immediate CS if: active heavy bleeding, fetal distress, or she goes into labor. Mode of delivery: always CS for placenta previa (vaginal delivery is contraindicated).
Warn about risk of placenta accreta (especially with multiple previous CS scars) and possible hysterectomy.

Preeclampsia Cascade — From Booking to Eclampsia
History Management
A 35-year-old primigravida presents at booking (12 weeks) with BP 151/95, nothing else remarkable. Follow her through her pregnancy.
1
Diagnosis at booking? What do you do beyond routine investigations?
✓ AnswerChronic Hypertension (HTN before 20 weeks or pre-pregnancy). Order internal medicine consultation. Start Methyldopa (safest antihypertensive in early pregnancy). Consider low-dose aspirin 75mg from 12 weeks.
2
At 32wk: BP 150/90, protein +1. New diagnosis?
✓ AnswerChronic HTN with Superimposed Preeclampsia. Management: Admit • PET profile (CBC, KFT, LFT, LDH, uric acid) • 24h urine protein collection • Steroids (dexamethasone) if <34wk • Fetal wellbeing (CTG + US Doppler)
3
At 36wk: BP 160/110, symptomatic. Diagnosis and management?
✓ AnswerSevere Preeclampsia.
Management:
1. Stabilize: MgSO4 (4g IV bolus → 1g/h × 24h) for seizure prophylaxis
2. Antihypertensive: IV Labetalol (1st) or IV Hydralazine (2nd) — target DBP 90–100
3. Deliver immediately (aim vaginal; CS for obstetric indications only)
4
She starts seizing. Eclampsia management steps?
✓ Answer1. Protect from injury — lateral position, padded environment
2. Clear airway + high-flow O₂ mask
3. IV access + Foley catheter
4. Blood + urine samples
5. MgSO4 loading dose
6. Antihypertensive agents
7. DO NOT deliver during acute seizure — stabilize first
8. Once: hypoxia corrected + seizures controlled + DBP 90–100 → deliver (prefer vaginal)
9. Continue MgSO4 24–48h after last fit. Fluid restrict 1ml/kg/h
5
MgSO4 toxicity: early signs and antidote?
✓ AnswerEarly: warmth, flushing → Loss of patellar reflex (first warning sign — check every 4h) → Somnolence/slurred speech → Respiratory depression → Cardiac arrest
Antidote: IV Calcium Gluconate 1g bolus immediately

Primary PPH — Atonic Uterus
Management
G6P6, hypertensive, delivered a 4.2 kg baby. 3 hours post-delivery develops heavy vaginal bleeding, estimated 1L. Uterus is boggy, above umbilicus.
1
Risk factors for PPH in this patient?
✓ AnswerGrand multiparity (G6P6) • Macrosomic baby (4.2 kg) • HTN (associated with atony) • Any previous PPH • Others: prolonged labor, overdistension (polyhydramnios, twins), oxytocin use, infection, fibroids
2
Most likely cause? How does examination confirm?
✓ AnswerUterine atony (MC cause, 80%). Confirmed by: boggy/soft uterus above umbilicus (fails to contract), large for normal. Also check: vagina/cervix for lacerations (trauma 20%), inspect placenta for missing cotyledons (retained tissue 5%), observe for absence of clots (coagulopathy)
3
Stepwise medical management of uterine atony?
✓ Answer1. Uterine massage + catheterize bladder
2. Oxytocin 5U IV bolus + 20U in 1L NS infusion
3. Bimanual compression
4. Ergonovine 200mcg IM (CI: HTN — in this patient, CAUTION)
5. Carboprost (PGF2α) 250mcg IM (CI: asthma — this patient has HTN not asthma, can use)
6. Misoprostol 1000mcg rectally (heat-stable, cheap)
4
Medical treatment failing. Surgical options in order?
✓ AnswerBakri balloon (tamponade) → UAE (if stable) → B-Lynch suture (compresses uterus, fertility-preserving) → Bilateral uterine artery ligation → Bilateral internal iliac artery ligation → Hysterectomy (lifesaving, last resort)
5
Resuscitation: blood products rationale?
✓ Answer2× large-bore IV (gauge 14) • Crystalloids 5L per 1L loss • PRBCs if blood loss >1.5L
For every 5 units PRBCs → give 1 unit FFP (prevent dilutional coagulopathy)
Cryoprecipitate if fibrinogen depleted • Platelets if <50×10⁹/L + active bleeding
1 unit PRBC = ↑Hb by 1g/dL

PROM at 34 Weeks — Take History & Manage
History Management
A 27-year-old G2P1, 34 weeks gestation, presents with a gush of watery vaginal discharge for 3 hours. No abdominal pain. How do you manage?
1
History of the discharge — what to ask?
✓ AnswerAmount, color, smell, duration/onset • Was it a gush (PROM) or dribbling (SUI)? • Any blood (abruption)? • Fetal movements (fetal wellbeing) • Abdominal pain/contractions • Fever (chorioamnionitis) • Previous PTL/PROM
2
Diagnosis? How to confirm?
✓ AnswerPreterm PROM (PPROM). Confirm with Speculum examination — visualize pooling of amniotic fluid in the posterior vaginal fornix. If unclear: Nitrazine test (amniotic fluid is alkaline → turns black). High vaginal swab for GBS.
3
Fetal and maternal monitoring?
✓ AnswerMaternal: Vital signs q6h (temp, HR, BP), CBC + CRP (chorioamnionitis markers)
Fetal: CTG (↑FHR may indicate infection), US (oligohydramnios, lie, presentation, biophysical profile)
4
No contractions, fetal heart good, CRP negative. Management plan?
✓ AnswerExpectant management:
1. Steroids (betamethasone 12mg IM q24h × 2) — patient is <34wk? Debatable at 34wk but borderline
2. Erythromycin 250mg QID × 10 days (improves neonatal outcomes in PPROM)
3. Observe for chorioamnionitis (temp, maternal/fetal tachycardia, uterine tenderness)
4. Fetal wellbeing monitoring
5
5 days later: temp 37.8°C, pulse 110, uterus tender. What now?
✓ AnswerChorioamnionitis — two main steps:
1. Switch to IV antibiotics (broad-spectrum: Ampicillin + Gentamicin or Metronidazole)
2. Deliver immediately
Bishop score 10 → Syntocinon infusion (favorable cervix, no need for prostaglandins) + continuous CTG

Postmenopausal Bleeding — 57-year-old
History Management
A 57-year-old P5 woman with 14 months of amenorrhea presents with vaginal bleeding for 2 months. How do you take her history and manage?
1
6 relevant history questions?
✓ Answer1. Introduce yourself + permission
2. Parity + previous menstrual cycles + age of menopause
3. Analyze bleeding: onset, amount, duration, color, clots, relation to intercourse
4. Associated symptoms: pelvic pain, discharge, weight loss, hot flushes, vaginal dryness
5. Drug history: anticoagulants, tamoxifen, HRT (especially unopposed estrogen)
6. Past history: DM, HTN, thyroid; personal/family history of cancers (endometrial, ovarian, breast, colon); last pap smear
2
4 physical examinations?
✓ Answer1. Abdominal exam (masses, ascites)
2. Speculum exam (cervical lesions, polyps, vaginal atrophy, source of bleeding)
3. Bimanual exam (uterine/adnexal masses, uterine size)
4. Lymph node exam (inguinal + supraclavicular)
3
3 important investigations?
✓ Answer1. Ultrasound — endometrial thickness (normal <4mm post-menopause; >4mm → biopsy)
2. Hysteroscopy-guided endometrial biopsy (gold standard for diagnosis)
3. Pap smear (cervical cause)
4
She asks about HRT. What type?
✓ AnswerShe has an intact uterus → Combined HRT (Estrogen + Progesterone). Progesterone is essential to protect the endometrium from unopposed estrogen → prevents endometrial hyperplasia/cancer. Estrogen-only HRT is only for hysterectomized women. Even local estrogen was not accepted by some examiners — this is a key point.

Early Pregnancy Bleeding + Ectopic
History Management
A 25-year-old P1+0, LMP 8 weeks ago, presents with lower abdominal pain and mild vaginal bleeding. US shows 2cm right adnexal mass and moderate free fluid in the pelvis. β-HCG = 3333 mIU/mL.
1
Diagnosis? Most appropriate immediate action?
✓ AnswerEctopic pregnancy with hemoperitoneum (suspected rupture).
Most appropriate immediate action: Take patient to theater for diagnostic laparoscopy.
Rationale: Free fluid in pelvis = hemoperitoneum = contraindication to MTX. Even though β-HCG <5000 and sac <4cm, the presence of free fluid mandates surgery.
2
Why can't you use MTX here? Why not just admit and repeat β-HCG?
✓ AnswerFree fluid (hemoperitoneum) is a direct CI to MTX — it indicates active bleeding/rupture. Repeating β-HCG would waste time with an actively bleeding patient. MTX is only for hemodynamically stable patients with NO free fluid, no cardiac activity, sac ≤4cm, β-HCG <5000.
3
Risk factors in this patient?
✓ AnswerNo risk factors mentioned → she is P1+0, could have had PID previously. Ask about: previous ectopic, PID history, tubal surgery, IUD use, previous pelvic surgery, infertility, ART/IVF, smoking.
4
At surgery: unruptured ectopic, other tube is healthy, patient doesn't want more children. Best surgical option?
✓ AnswerSalpingectomy (removal of the whole affected tube). Rationale: patient doesn't desire future fertility + other tube is healthy + salpingectomy has lower risk of persistent trophoblastic tissue. Salpingostomy would be preferred if other tube is damaged or fertility desired.
5
Post-operatively, she is Rh-negative. What do you do?
✓ AnswerGive Anti-D 500 IU IM within 72 hours. Ectopic pregnancy is a sensitizing event (causes feto-maternal hemorrhage). Anti-D is indicated for all Rh-negative women with ectopic pregnancy, regardless of gestational age. Also advise to avoid pregnancy for at least 3 months.

Infertility — Couple Unable to Conceive for 3 Years
History Management
A 27-year-old P1, married for 5 years, unable to conceive for 3 years. Her husband is reportedly healthy. Her pelvic US is normal. How do you manage this couple?
1
Diagnosis?
✓ AnswerSecondary infertility (she has had a previous pregnancy — P1). Primary infertility = no previous pregnancy ever.
2
Two initial investigations?
✓ Answer1. Semen Fluid Analysis (SFA) — must be done first, quick, non-invasive
2. Assessment of tubal patency — HSG (initial) or Laparoscopy + methylene blue dye (more accurate, therapeutic)
3
SFA results: volume 0.5ml, count 1 million/ml, motility 20%, morphology 35%. Interpretation?
✓ AnswerALL abnormal:
Volume: 0.5ml (normal >2ml) → LOW
Count: 1 million (normal >20 million/ml) → SEVERE oligospermia
Motility: 20% (normal >50% forward motility) → LOW
Morphology: 35% (normal >40%) → borderline low
Diagnosis: Oligoasthenoteratospermia
4
How to confirm ovulation? What investigation?
✓ AnswerDay-21 serum progesterone (mid-luteal phase). A level >30 nmol/L (or >10 ng/mL) confirms ovulation. Also: FSH:LH ratio for PCOS diagnosis.
5
Laparoscopy shows bilateral tubal blockage. 2 management options?
✓ Answer1. Tubal surgery (recanalization via laparoscopy or selective HSG — if proximal block)
2. IVF (bypasses tubes entirely; best option for bilateral complete blockage)
If male factor severe: ICSI (Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection)

Obstetric Examination — How to Perform & Summarize
Examination
G2P1, 37 weeks gestation. Perform and describe the obstetric examination. Findings: SFH 35cm, singleton, breech presentation, back to left, not engaged.
1
Steps of obstetric examination — what to say and do?
✓ Answer1. Introduce + consent + privacy + exposure (xiphisternum to symphysis)
2. General inspection: hands (edema, anemia), face (edema), vital signs, weight
3. Inspection of abdomen: shape, striae, linea nigra, movements, scars
4. Superficial palpation: tenderness, guarding, contractions
5. Fundal height: tape measure pubic symphysis to fundus in cm
6. Leopold's maneuvers: lie → presentation → position (back) → engagement
7. Auscultation: fetal heart with Doppler/Pinard
8. End: "I would also perform a pelvic examination"
2
How to summarize findings (say this out loud at the end)?
✓ Answer"SFH is 35 cm. This is a singleton pregnancy, with a longitudinal lie, breech presentation, back to the left, and the presenting part is not engaged (5/5 palpable abdominally). Fetal heart rate is [X] bpm. The patient is/is not in labor based on [contractions/no contractions]."
3
Breech at 37 weeks — when and how do you offer ECV?
✓ AnswerECV offered at ≥37 weeks. Prerequisites: Normal CTG, normal AFI, placenta not previa, no previous CS (relative CI), singleton.
Success rate: ~50%.
Complications: PROM, cord prolapse/compression, abruption, fetal distress, fetomaternal hemorrhage → give Anti-D if Rh-negative after ECV.
4
ECV fails. Management options?
✓ Answer1. Elective CS at 38–39 weeks (safest for mother and baby)
2. Vaginal breech delivery (criteria: frank breech, adequate pelvis, experienced obstetrician, fetal head not hyperextended, fetal weight 1.5–3.5kg, no other complications)
At your level — exam answer: recommend CS after failed ECV.

Contraception Counselling — Lactating Woman asking for Mini-Pill
Counselling
P1, breastfeeding, 6 weeks postpartum. Comes to clinic asking about the mini pill (Progestin-Only Pill). Counsel her.
1
How does she take it?
✓ AnswerTake one tablet daily every 28 days with NO pill-free interval (unlike COC which has 7 days off). Must be taken at the same time each day. Start on day 1 of menses or immediately postpartum/postabortion.
2
Side effects?
✓ AnswerVaginal spotting/irregular bleeding (MC) • Functional ovarian cysts • Acne • Hirsutism • Breast tenderness • Amenorrhea (may occur, not harmful). Failure rate: 2–3%.
3
Missing a pill — what to do?
✓ AnswerPOP: 3-hour window (vs COC 12-hour window). If >3 hours late: take the missed pill immediately + today's pill on time + use backup contraception (condom) for 48 hours. Much stricter than COC.
4
Pregnancy after stopping? What to warn about?
✓ AnswerFertility returns immediately after stopping — no waiting period needed, no amenorrhea phase expected. If she becomes pregnant while on POP → increased risk of ECTOPIC PREGNANCY (because progesterone slows tubal motility). Warn her to do a pregnancy test and seek care early if she misses pills.
5
Why is POP preferred over COC for breastfeeding?
✓ AnswerCOC (containing estrogen) may reduce milk production and the quality of breast milk. POP (progestin-only) does not affect milk supply and is safe for the breastfed infant. COC is contraindicated within 6 months postpartum while breastfeeding.
